# **How to Write an Executive Summary**

An executive summary is a brief overview at the beginning of a business plan, pitch deck, or report. It is designed to summarize the entire document, highlighting the most critical information so that busy investors, executives, or stakeholders can understand the core of your business without reading the full plan.

## **What to Include in an Executive Summary**

While the exact components can vary depending on your business type and your audience, a standard executive summary should include the following sections:

* **The Hook:** A compelling opening statement that captures the reader's attention and explains the core mission of your business.  
* **Company Description & Team:** A brief overview of your company, when it was founded, who the founders/key team members are, and their relevant expertise.  
* **The Problem (Market Analysis):** The specific pain point or gap in the market that you have identified.  
* **The Solution (Product or Service):** How your business, product, or service directly solves the problem you identified.  
* **Financials & Projections:** A high-level summary of your financial health, current revenue (if any), and future financial projections.  
* **The Ask (Funding Request):** If you are pitching to investors, clearly state how much money you are looking to raise and how you plan to use it.

## **How to Write an Executive Summary in 6 Steps**

### **1\. Write It Last**

Even though the executive summary appears first in your business plan, you should write it last. You cannot accurately summarize a document that hasn't been completely fleshed out yet. Finish your business plan first, then pull the key points to form the summary.

### **2\. Keep It Short and Engaging**

Investors and executives are busy. Aim to keep your executive summary under two pages. It should be concise, scannable, and directly to the point.

### **3\. Start with a Hook**

Your first paragraph needs to grab the reader's attention immediately. State clearly what your business does and why it matters in an engaging way.

### **4\. Focus on the Main Points**

Don't get bogged down in the minutiae. Highlight the most crucial data points, your unique value proposition, and the most compelling financial projections. Leave the deep dive for the actual business plan.

### **5\. Proofread and Format**

Ensure your summary is easy to read. Use bullet points, bold text for key metrics, and short paragraphs. Double-check for any grammatical errors or typos, as this is your first impression.

### **6\. Tailor it to Your Audience**

Adapt the tone and focus of your executive summary depending on who is reading it. An angel investor might care more about the team and the exit strategy, while a bank lending officer will focus heavily on financial stability and cash flow.

## **Common Mistakes to Avoid**

* **Making it too long:** If it exceeds two pages, it is no longer a summary.  
* **Copying and pasting:** Don't just copy paragraphs directly from the main business plan. Synthesize the information into a fresh, cohesive narrative.  
* **Using too much jargon:** Keep the language accessible. Avoid dense industry terminology that an outside investor might not understand.  
* **Forgetting the financials:** Even if your product is revolutionary, investors want to see the math. Always include high-level financial data.  
* **Sounding unrealistic:** Be optimistic but grounded. Avoid claiming you have "no competition" or guaranteeing impossible returns.

## **Executive Summary Template Structure**

If you're starting from scratch, you can use this simple framework to structure your summary:

1. **Introduction:** (1 paragraph) Hook the reader and state your business name, location, and core mission.  
2. **Company & Team:** (1 paragraph) Who are you, and why are you the right people to build this?  
3. **Market & Problem:** (1 paragraph) What is the specific market need?  
4. **Product & Solution:** (1-2 paragraphs) What are you selling, and why is it better than current alternatives?  
5. **Financial Overview:** (Bullet points) Current sales, projected revenue, margins, etc.  
6. **The Ask:** (1 sentence/paragraph) How much capital do you need, and what milestone will it help you achieve?
